# grace hopper

![Portrait](images/portrait.jpg)
*Rear Admiral Grace Hopper — the "Queen of Code" who told computers to speak English, and told everyone else to stop saying "we've always done it this way."*

**Field:** Programming Languages, Compilers

- **Lifespan:** 1906–1992
- **Key contribution:** Developed first compilers; co-created COBOL; pioneered machine-independent programming
- **Impact:** Made programming accessible by proving computers could understand English-like instructions

## Biography

Born Grace Brewster Murray on December 9, 1906, in New York City. Curious as a child — she dismantled **seven** alarm clocks at age 7 just to figure out how they worked. (Her mother, understandably, limited her to one after that.)

She earned her PhD in mathematics from Yale in 1934, making her one of very few women holding a math doctorate at the time. But academic life alone wasn't enough for Grace. When WWII broke out, she joined the United States Navy Reserve at the age of 37, despite being told she was too old and too light (she weighed 105 pounds — 15 under the minimum). She got a waiver, because of course she did.

Assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard, she became one of the first programmers of the Harvard Mark I — a 51-foot-long, 5-ton electromechanical computer. She later said: *"It was an impressive beast."*

After the war, she continued working on cutting-edge computing at Eckert–Mauchly and its successor companies, where she did her most groundbreaking work on compilers and programming languages.

The Navy kept trying to retire her, and she kept coming back. She was recalled to active duty in 1967 at age 60 for what was supposed to be a six-month stint — and stayed for **19 more years**. She finally retired as a Rear Admiral in 1986 at the age of 79, making her the oldest active-duty commissioned officer in the United States Navy at the time. The retirement ceremony was held aboard the USS Constitution, the oldest commissioned warship afloat. Fitting.

## Major Contributions

### 1. The First Compiler — A-0 System (1952)
Grace wrote the first compiler, the A-0 System, which translated mathematical notation into machine code. When she told people what she'd done, nobody believed her. *"I had a running compiler and nobody would touch it. They told me computers could only do arithmetic."* She spent years convincing skeptics that computers could, in fact, do more than crunch numbers.

### 2. COBOL (1959)
Grace was a driving force behind COBOL (COmmon Business-Oriented Language), an English-like programming language designed for business applications. Many "real programmers" scoffed at the idea of a language that looked like English. Decades later, COBOL still processes an estimated **95% of ATM transactions** and **80% of in-person transactions** worldwide. Who's laughing now?

### 3. The First Computer "Bug" (1947)
While working on the Harvard Mark II, her team found a moth stuck in a relay, causing a malfunction. They taped it into the logbook with the note: *"First actual case of bug being found."* The term "debugging" had been used loosely before, but Grace and her team gave it a literal, physical specimen. That logbook page is now in the Smithsonian.

### 4. Machine-Independent Programming Languages
Grace pioneered the concept that programming languages should be independent of the hardware they run on. This seems obvious today, but at the time, each computer had its own unique language. Her vision laid the groundwork for portable software — the reason you can write code once and run it on different machines.

### 5. "It's Easier to Ask Forgiveness Than Permission"
She didn't just coin a programming philosophy — she lived it. Grace was famous for acting first and getting approval later, an attitude that drove bureaucrats nuts but resulted in some of the most important innovations in computing history.

## Fun Facts

- 🧵 She carried a **"nanosecond"** everywhere she went — an 11.8-inch piece of wire representing the maximum distance light can travel in one billionth of a second. She used it to explain to generals and admirals why satellite communication has latency. She also handed out "microseconds" (about 984 feet of wire) and joked that some people's programs had "too many microseconds."
- 📺 She appeared on **Late Night with David Letterman** in 1986, charming the audience while explaining nanoseconds to Dave.
- 🚢 The Navy named a guided-missile destroyer after her — the **USS Hopper (DDG-70)**. Its motto? *"Aude et Effice"* — "Dare and Do." Very on-brand.
- 🎂 She was born on **December 9**, which is now celebrated as **Grace Hopper Day** in parts of the tech community.
- 🏅 She received the **Presidential Medal of Freedom** posthumously in 2016 from President Obama.
- 🪳 She once said the Mark I sounded like *"a million knitting needles"* clicking away.

## Famous Quotes

> *"The most dangerous phrase in the language is, 'We've always done it this way.'"*

> *"It's easier to ask forgiveness than it is to get permission."*

> *"A ship in port is safe, but that's not what ships are built for."*

> *"If it's a good idea, go ahead and do it. It's much easier to apologize than it is to get permission."*

> *"Humans are allergic to change. They love to say, 'We've always done it this way.' I try to fight that."*
