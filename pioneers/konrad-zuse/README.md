# konrad zuse

![Portrait](images/portrait.jpg)
*Konrad Zuse — the German engineer who quietly built the world's first programmable computer in his parents' living room.*

**Field:** Computer Hardware, Early Computing

- **Lifespan:** 1910–1995
- **Key contribution:** Built the Z3 — the world's first working programmable, fully automatic digital computer
- **Impact:** Proved that programmable digital computing was physically possible; also created the first high-level programming language concept

## Biography

Konrad Zuse was a German civil engineer, inventor, and computer pioneer. While the British had Turing and the Americans had von Neumann, Germany had Zuse — a man who built the world's first functioning programmable computer largely by himself, in his parents' apartment, during World War II, using scavenged telephone relay switches and old movie film for data storage.

If that's not the most badass engineering origin story ever, I don't know what is.

### Early Life

- Born on June 22, 1910, in Berlin, Germany
- Grew up in Braunsberg, East Prussia (now Braniewo, Poland)
- As a child, he was interested in both engineering and art — he was a talented illustrator and painter throughout his life
- Studied civil engineering at the Technische Hochschule Berlin-Charlottenburg (now TU Berlin)
- During his studies, he became frustrated with the tedious manual calculations required in structural engineering. Rather than just complaining about it (like a normal person), he decided to build a machine to do the calculations automatically.

### Career

- **1936:** Left his job at the Henschel aircraft factory to build a computing machine full-time. In his parents' living room. His mother was reportedly very supportive, even though her apartment was filling up with mechanical parts.
- **1938:** Completed the **Z1** — a mechanical binary calculator. It worked in theory but was unreliable due to the precision limitations of hand-built mechanical parts.
- **1940:** Built the **Z2** using telephone relays instead of mechanical parts, improving reliability
- **1941:** Completed the **Z3** — the world's first working programmable, fully automatic digital computer. It used binary arithmetic, floating-point numbers, and was controlled by punched film.
- **1942–1945:** Worked on the **Z4** while also developing **Plankalkül** (Plan Calculus), the first high-level programming language concept
- **1945:** The Z3 was destroyed in an Allied bombing raid on Berlin. The Z4 survived because Zuse evacuated it to the Alpine village of Hinterstein.
- **1949:** Founded **Zuse KG**, one of the earliest commercial computer companies

### Later Life & Legacy

- Zuse KG built a series of computers (Z5 through Z43) and became a successful company
- **1967:** Sold Zuse KG to Siemens, which eventually merged its computing division with other companies
- Spent his later years painting (he was genuinely talented) and advocating for recognition of his computing contributions
- For decades, his work was largely unknown outside Germany, partly due to wartime isolation and partly because the British and American computing stories dominated the narrative
- Died on December 18, 1995, in Hünfeld, Germany
- Today, he is widely recognized as having built the first functional programmable computer

## Major Contributions

### The Z3 — First Working Programmable Computer

The machine that quietly changed history in a Berlin workshop.

**Year(s):** 1941

**Context:** Zuse wanted to automate the tedious calculations of civil engineering. Unlike many computing pioneers who came from academic backgrounds, Zuse was a practical engineer solving a practical problem — with extraordinary results.

**Technical Details:**
- Used approximately 2,600 telephone relays
- Operated in binary (base-2) — a crucial design decision
- Supported floating-point arithmetic with a 22-bit word length (7-bit exponent, 14-bit mantissa, 1 sign bit)
- Could perform addition in 0.8 seconds and multiplication in 3 seconds
- Programs were fed via punched strips of discarded 35mm movie film
- Could store 64 numbers in memory
- Supported arithmetic operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and square root
- Operated at a clock speed of about 5.33 Hz (yes, hertz — not megahertz, not gigahertz... hertz)

**Impact:** The Z3 has been shown to be theoretically Turing-complete (though Zuse didn't know about Turing's work at the time — wartime isolation). It was the first machine to actually work as a programmable, automatic digital computer. While the British Colossus and American ENIAC are more famous, they came later (1943 and 1945 respectively), and Colossus was not general-purpose.

### Plankalkül — The First High-Level Programming Language

Zuse didn't just build computers — he also thought about how to program them properly.

**Year(s):** 1942–1945 (not published until 1972)

**Context:** While working on the Z4 during the war, Zuse began developing a formal notation for expressing algorithms — essentially a programming language.

**Technical Details:**
- Name means "Plan Calculus" or "Planning Calculation" in German
- Supported arrays, records (structures), floating-point arithmetic, and assertions
- Included conditional statements and iterative loops
- Had a two-dimensional notation (subscripts written below the main line)
- Zuse even wrote a Plankalkül program to play chess

**Impact:** Although Plankalkül was never implemented during Zuse's active career and wasn't published until 1972, it predates FORTRAN (1957) and every other high-level programming language. If it had been published and implemented earlier, the history of programming languages might look very different. A working implementation was finally created in 2000 at the Free University of Berlin.

### The Z1 — First Binary Mechanical Computer

**Year(s):** 1936–1938

**Context:** Zuse's first attempt at building a computing machine, constructed in his parents' living room.

**Technical Details:**
- Entirely mechanical — built from hand-cut metal plates
- Used binary representation (a bold choice when most computing efforts used decimal)
- Had a 64-word floating-point memory
- Input via punched 35mm movie film
- Unreliable due to the difficulty of manufacturing precision mechanical parts by hand

**Impact:** While the Z1 didn't work reliably, it established the fundamental design principles that Zuse would refine in the Z2 and Z3. His independent adoption of binary computing was ahead of its time.

## Influence & Legacy

### Direct Impact
- Built the world's first working programmable, automatic digital computer (Z3, 1941)
- Conceived the first high-level programming language (Plankalkül)
- Founded one of the first commercial computer companies (Zuse KG)
- Pioneered binary floating-point arithmetic in computer design

### Indirect Impact
- His work demonstrated that programmable digital computing was physically achievable
- His concept of digital physics (*Rechnender Raum*) anticipated ideas later explored by Stephen Wolfram and others
- Inspired European computing development, particularly in Germany

### Modern Relevance
- The Z3 is now recognized as a landmark in computing history
- Binary floating-point arithmetic, which Zuse pioneered, is the standard in all modern processors (IEEE 754 standard)
- Replicas of the Z1 and Z3 are displayed at the Deutsches Technikmuseum in Berlin
- His digital physics ideas continue to be discussed in theoretical computer science and physics
