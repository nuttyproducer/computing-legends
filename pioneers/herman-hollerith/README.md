# herman hollerith

![Portrait](images/portrait.jpg)
*Herman Hollerith — the man who turned the U.S. Census into a data processing revolution and accidentally founded IBM.*

**Field:** Data Processing, Business Machines

- **Lifespan:** 1860–1929
- **Key contribution:** Invented the electromechanical punch card tabulating machine
- **Impact:** Automated data processing; his company became IBM

## Biography

Herman Hollerith was an American inventor and businessman who developed the first successful electromechanical punch card tabulating system. His machines were used to process the 1890 U.S. Census in just one year — the 1880 census had taken eight. He then founded the Tabulating Machine Company, which eventually merged with other companies to become International Business Machines Corporation — better known as **IBM**.

Not bad for a guy who got the idea from watching a train conductor punch tickets.

### Early Life

- Born on February 29, 1860, in Buffalo, New York (yes, he was a leap year baby — technically only had a birthday every four years)
- Son of German immigrants
- Attended the City College of New York and then the Columbia School of Mines (now part of Columbia University), graduating in 1879 at age 19
- Immediately went to work at the U.S. Census Bureau, where he witnessed firsthand the enormous difficulty of processing census data manually
- The 1880 census took nearly eight years to process — by the time it was done, the data was already outdated. This problem stuck with Hollerith.

### Career

- **1882:** Became an instructor at MIT, but continued thinking about the census problem
- **1884:** Filed his first patent for a punch card tabulating system — inspired by watching railroad conductors punch tickets in specific positions to record passenger descriptions ("punch photography")
- **1889:** His tabulating machines were selected for the 1890 U.S. Census after a competitive trial against two other systems — Hollerith's won handily
- **1890:** The census was completed in approximately one year using his machines, saving the government an estimated $5 million (about $170 million in today's money)
- **1896:** Founded the Tabulating Machine Company to sell his machines commercially
- Clients included railroads, insurance companies, and governments worldwide
- His machines were used for censuses in Austria, Canada, Norway, Russia, and many other countries

### Later Life & Legacy

- Had a famously difficult personality and charged high prices for his machines and punch cards (he basically invented the "razors and blades" business model — sell the machine, charge for the cards)
- The Census Bureau, frustrated by his pricing, developed its own machines for the 1910 census using James Powers' competing design
- **1911:** Sold his company, which merged with others to form the Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company (CTR)
- **1924:** CTR was renamed **International Business Machines Corporation (IBM)** under Thomas J. Watson Sr.
- Hollerith retired to a farm in Maryland, where he raised Guernsey cattle
- Died on November 17, 1929, in Washington, D.C.

## Major Contributions

### Electromechanical Punch Card Tabulating System

The machine that launched the data processing industry.

**Year(s):** 1884–1890

**Context:** Processing the U.S. Census by hand was becoming untenable. The country was growing rapidly, and the 1880 census took eight years to tabulate. At that rate, the 1890 census wouldn't be finished before the 1900 census needed to start.

**Technical Details:**
- Data was encoded by punching holes in specific positions on cards (each card represented one person)
- Cards were placed on a reader with a mercury-filled cup beneath each potential hole position
- Spring-loaded pins would pass through the punched holes, making contact with the mercury and completing an electrical circuit
- Each completed circuit advanced a counting dial by one and could also trigger the opening of a specific sorting bin lid
- The system could tabulate approximately 80 cards per minute with sorting

**Impact:** Reduced census processing time from eight years to one year. Proved that large-scale data processing could be mechanized. Created an entirely new industry — data processing — and the company that would dominate it for decades (IBM).

### The Punch Card Standard

Hollerith's card format became the industry standard for decades.

**Year(s):** 1890s onwards

**Context:** Hollerith needed a durable, standardized medium for encoding data.

**Technical Details:**
- Originally based on the size of the U.S. dollar bill at the time (so they could be stored in existing Treasury Department containers — practical thinking!)
- The standard Hollerith card had 12 rows and up to 80 columns
- This "80-column" standard persisted for decades — it's the reason old computer terminals had 80-column displays, and why many coding style guides still recommend 80-character line limits today
- The cards were made of stiff paper and could be machine-sorted, counted, and tabulated

**Impact:** Punch cards remained the primary input medium for computers well into the 1970s. The 80-column standard influenced terminal displays, text formatting, and coding conventions that persist to this day.

## Influence & Legacy

### Direct Impact
- Invented the electromechanical tabulating machine — the ancestor of all data processing systems
- Created the punch card as a data storage medium (used for nearly a century)
- Founded the company that became IBM — one of the most influential technology corporations in history
- Pioneered the concept of data processing as a service industry

### Indirect Impact
- The 80-column card standard influenced computing for decades (terminals, text formatting, coding style)
- Established the model of selling/leasing machines and charging for consumable media (punch cards)
- Demonstrated that government-scale data problems could be solved with machines
- His work inspired the development of increasingly sophisticated tabulating and computing machines

### Modern Relevance
- IBM, his corporate descendant, remains a major technology company
- The concept of encoding data in machine-readable formats traces directly back to his punch cards
- The 80-character line convention in code editors is a living fossil of his card format
- His approach to data processing foreshadowed modern big data and analytics
