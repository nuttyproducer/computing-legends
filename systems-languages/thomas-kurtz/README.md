# thomas e. kurtz

![Portrait](images/portrait.jpg)
_Thomas E. Kurtz — Co-creator of BASIC with John Kemeny, the language that brought programming to the masses. His vision of computing for everyone became reality when BASIC shipped with every personal computer._

**Field:** Programming Languages

- **Lifespan:** b. 1928
- **Key contribution:** Co-creator of BASIC programming language, pioneer of time-sharing systems
- **Impact:** Made programming accessible to millions, influenced every home computer of the 1970s-80s

## Biography

### Early Life & Education

Born Thomas Eugene Kurtz in Oak Park, Illinois in 1928. Studied mathematics at Knox College (B.A. 1950). Earned M.A. (1956) and Ph.D. (1956) in statistics from Princeton University. His doctoral work focused on multiple comparisons in mathematical statistics.

### Career

Joined Dartmouth College mathematics department in 1956. Met John Kemeny, who shared his vision that computing should be accessible to all students, not just scientists and engineers. In the early 1960s, they secured NSF funding to develop time-sharing and make computing interactive.

Co-created BASIC (Beginner's All-purpose Symbolic Instruction Code) with Kemeny in 1964. The first BASIC program ran at 4 a.m. on May 1, 1964 — Kurtz was there to witness it. The language was designed to be learned in one class session and was given away for free.

Served as Director of the Kiewit Computation Center at Dartmouth (1966-1975), overseeing the expansion of the time-sharing system. Under his leadership, Dartmouth became a model for computing education worldwide.

Co-founded True BASIC Inc. (1983) with Kemeny to develop and promote a standardized, structured version of BASIC that fixed problems with the many divergent dialects that had emerged.

### Later Life

Professor emeritus at Dartmouth. Continued advocating for computing education and open access to technology. Still alive and active in computing history preservation.

## Major Contributions

### 1. BASIC Programming Language (1964)

- **Year:** 1964
- **Context:** Computing was intimidating and inaccessible. Kurtz and Kemeny wanted to change that.
- **Technical Details:** Designed for beginners: simple syntax, immediate feedback, English-like commands. Interpreted, not compiled. First program: `10 PRINT "HELLO" 20 END` — that's it. No complex setup, no declarations.
- **Impact:** BASIC became the first language for millions. Every home computer (Apple II, Commodore 64, TRS-80, IBM PC) shipped with BASIC. Microsoft's first product was BASIC. Visual Basic later dominated business software.

### 2. Dartmouth Time-Sharing System (DTSS)

- **Year:** 1963-1964
- **Context:** Computers ran batch jobs — submit cards, wait hours for results
- **Technical Details:** Built one of the first general-purpose time-sharing systems. Allowed hundreds of students to use terminals simultaneously. Response time under 2 seconds. Revolutionary for 1960s.
- **Impact:** Proved interactive computing was practical. Model for modern multi-user operating systems.

### 3. True BASIC (1983)

- **Year:** 1983
- **Context:** BASIC had fragmented into incompatible dialects. Programs couldn't run across different computers.
- **Technical Details:** Structured BASIC with modern control structures (no line numbers required), proper scoping, graphics, sound. Fully portable across platforms.
- **Impact:** Influenced Visual Basic and modern BASIC variants. Showed that BASIC could evolve beyond its simple origins.

## Influence & Legacy

### Direct Influence

BASIC shaped an entire generation of programmers. Bill Gates and Paul Allen's first product was Altair BASIC (1975) — this became Microsoft BASIC, which shipped with DOS and made Microsoft's fortune. Steve Wozniak wrote Integer BASIC for the Apple II. Every programmer who learned on a Commodore 64, TRS-80, or early IBM PC learned BASIC first.

### Indirect Influence

Kurtz proved that computing should be accessible to everyone, not just experts. The Dartmouth model — computing as a liberal arts tool — influenced universities worldwide. His advocacy for open access and free software predated the open-source movement by decades.

### Modern Relevance

While modern BASIC variants are less common, the philosophy lives on: Python's simplicity echoes BASIC's design goals. Scratch, used to teach millions of kids programming, follows BASIC's principle of instant feedback. Every "learn to code" initiative traces back to Kurtz and Kemeny's vision that programming should be for everyone.
