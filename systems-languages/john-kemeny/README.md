# john g. kemeny

![Portrait](images/portrait.jpg)
_John G. Kemeny — Hungarian refugee who escaped the Nazis, worked on the Manhattan Project, and co-created BASIC to make computing accessible to everyone. Later became president of Dartmouth College._

**Field:** Programming Languages

- **Lifespan:** 1926-1992
- **Key contribution:** Co-creator of BASIC programming language, pioneer of time-sharing systems
- **Impact:** Made programming accessible to millions, influenced every home computer of the 1970s-80s

## Biography

### Early Life & Education

Born János György Kemény in Budapest, Hungary in 1926. Jewish family fled Nazi Germany in 1940, arrived in New York when John was 14. Learned English in six months, excelled in mathematics. Entered Princeton at age 19. At 19, was drafted and assigned to the Manhattan Project at Los Alamos (1945) where he worked with Richard Feynman on calculations for the atomic bomb.

After the war, returned to Princeton, earned Ph.D. in mathematics (1949) under Alonzo Church. Worked as Albert Einstein's mathematical assistant for one year — one of the highlights of his life.

### Career

Joined Dartmouth College mathematics department in 1953. Became chairman of the math department in 1955. In the early 1960s, believed computers should be accessible to all students, not just scientists. Partnered with Tom Kurtz to make this vision real.

Co-created BASIC (Beginner's All-purpose Symbolic Instruction Code) in 1964 with a team of Dartmouth undergraduates. The language was designed to be simple enough for non-technical students to learn in a single afternoon. Released it for free — radical idea in 1960s when software was proprietary.

Also developed the Dartmouth Time-Sharing System (DTSS) — one of the first systems to allow multiple users to share a single computer simultaneously. By 1968, 80% of Dartmouth students were using computers regularly.

Served as Dartmouth's 13th president (1970-1981). Led the investigation into the Three Mile Island nuclear accident as chairman of the Kemeny Commission (1979) — his report led to major nuclear safety reforms.

### Later Life

Returned to teaching mathematics and computer science after presidency. Continued advocating for accessible technology and education reform until his death from heart disease in 1992.

## Major Contributions

### 1. BASIC Programming Language (1964)

- **Year:** 1964
- **Context:** Computing was restricted to experts. Kemeny believed everyone should learn programming.
- **Technical Details:** Designed for simplicity: line numbers, GOTO statements, simple English commands (PRINT, INPUT, IF...THEN). Interpreted, not compiled — immediate feedback. Free and open from the start.
- **Impact:** Every home computer (Apple II, Commodore 64, IBM PC, TRS-80) shipped with BASIC. Microsoft's first product was BASIC. Millions learned programming through BASIC. Visual Basic later became one of the most popular languages.

### 2. Dartmouth Time-Sharing System (DTSS)

- **Year:** 1963-1964
- **Context:** Computers were batch-processing machines. One person, one program at a time.
- **Technical Details:** Developed one of the first general-purpose time-sharing systems. Allowed hundreds of students to use the computer simultaneously through terminals. Inspired by MIT's CTSS but designed for education, not research.
- **Impact:** Proved interactive computing was practical and necessary. Model for all modern multi-user systems.

### 3. Democratizing Computing

- **Year:** 1960s-1980s
- **Context:** Computing was for elite scientists and mathematicians
- **Technical Details:** Made BASIC free. Encouraged distribution. Wrote accessible textbooks. Advocated for computing literacy in K-12 education.
- **Impact:** Transformed computing from exclusive to universal. Generation of programmers learned on BASIC.

## Influence & Legacy

### Direct Influence

BASIC became the first programming language for an entire generation. Bill Gates and Paul Allen wrote Microsoft BASIC as their first product. Steve Wozniak wrote Integer BASIC for the Apple II. Every personal computer of the 1970s-80s booted into BASIC. The language's simplicity inspired millions to become programmers.

### Indirect Influence

Proved that computing should be democratic and accessible. The Dartmouth model — interactive computing for all students — became the standard for universities worldwide. His advocacy for computing literacy influenced education policy for decades.

### Modern Relevance

While BASIC itself has faded, its legacy lives on: Visual Basic was ubiquitous in business software for decades. Python's simplicity echoes BASIC's philosophy. The idea that programming should be accessible to beginners — not just experts — is now universal. Every "learn to code" movement traces back to Kemeny's vision.
